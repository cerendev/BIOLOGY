🦎 1. Adaptations & Variations
DEFINITION

🧠 What is an Adaptation?

A structure, behavior, or physiological process that helps an organism survive & reproduce in its environment.

Adaptations come from genetic variations — if a variation helps survival, it gets passed down → becomes an adaptation!

EXAMPLES

📝 3 Types of Adaptation

  • 🎭 Camouflage – stick insect looks like a twig; hides from predators
  • 😴 Hibernation – bears reduce metabolism to survive winter (physiological)
  • 🦋 Mimicry – viceroy butterfly mimics toxic monarch; predators avoid both
KEY TERM

⚡ Selective Advantage

A genetic advantage that improves an organism's chance of survival in a changing environment.

Example: Some Daphnia (water fleas) mutated to survive in warm water (25–30°C) when others couldn't. That mutation = selective advantage!

EXAMPLE STORY

🦋 Peppered Moth Classic!

Before Industrial Revolution: Light moths survived on light lichen trees. Black moths were eaten.

After: Factories covered trees in soot → black moths hid, light moths got eaten! Black moths = 95% of population.

After clean-air laws: Light moths came back. Environment drives selection!

🔥 Exam Tip — Variation vs Adaptation

Variation = any difference between individuals (could be helpful, harmful, or neutral). Adaptation = a variation that has been "selected for" because it helps survival. Not all variations become adaptations!

🔬 2. The 4 Big Scientists
👴

George Cuvier

1769–1832

Father of Paleontology (study of fossils). Found each rock layer has unique fossils — deeper = more different from today. Species can go EXTINCT!

💥 Catastrophism

Earth changed through sudden violent events (floods, volcanoes).

🪨

Charles Lyell

Scottish Geologist

Rejected Cuvier. Said geological processes are slow & continuous, same as today. Earth must be MUCH older than 6000 years!

🌊 Uniformitarianism

"The present is the key to the past." Slow processes → big changes over time.

🦒

Jean-Baptiste Lamarck

1744–1829

Thought organisms get better over time. Body parts you USE a lot grow stronger, parts you don't use disappear. Then you PASS these to offspring.

🔄 Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics + Use & Disuse

Example: Giraffes stretched necks → passed long necks to babies. (WRONG but important!)

⛵

Charles Darwin

1809–1882

The big one! Observed Galapagos finches — each had different beak for different food. All from one ancestor!

🏆 Natural Selection = Survival of the Fittest

4 key points: overproduction → variation → struggle → survival of best-fit → traits passed on.

⚔️ Darwin vs Lamarck — The Big Comparison

Feature 🦒 Lamarck ⛵ Darwin
Mechanism Inheritance of acquired characteristics Natural Selection
How traits change Organism changes during its lifetime → passes it on Random variation exists → environment selects who survives
Giraffe neck? Giraffes stretched necks → offspring born with longer necks Some giraffes were born with longer necks → they survived better → reproduced more
Correct? ❌ Wrong — acquired traits can't be inherited genetically ✅ Correct!
🦴 3. Evidence for Evolution

🪨 1. Fossil Record & Stratum Layers

🐦 TOP = YOUNGEST (closest to today's species)
🦖 Middle layers → transitional species
🐟 Bottom = OLDEST (most different from today)
⬇️ DEEPER = MORE ANCIENT & SIMPLER

Cuvier discovered each stratum (rock layer) has unique fossils. Canada's Burgess Shale has Cambrian animals (500+ million years old)!

🌍 3. Biogeography

  • Island animals resemble nearby mainland animals (not same environment elsewhere)
  • Galapagos animals → from South America
  • Same fossils found on different continents that were once joined (Gondwana)
  • Related species rarely in exact same habitat

🦴 2. Transitional Fossils & Vestigial Structures

Transitional fossils = show intermediary link between 2 groups. Example: Ancient whales had tiny legs & pelvis → link to land ancestors!

Vestigial structures = reduced leftovers from ancestors that no longer have function. Examples: human appendix, wisdom teeth, goosebumps

💪 4 & 5. Anatomy & Embryology

Homologous structures = Same bones, different functions → inherited from common ancestor.
Example: human arm, cat paw, whale flipper, bat wing = same bones! 🦴

Analogous structures = Similar function, different origin. Example: bat wing vs butterfly wing vs bird wing — all fly but NOT related!

Embryology: All vertebrate embryos have gill pouches → fish keep them as gills, humans make ears/throat. Same early blueprint = common ancestor!

🧬 6. DNA Evidence

Similar DNA patterns = inherited from common ancestor. Dogs are related to bears. Whales & dolphins → related to hoofed animals (cows, deer)! The more similar the DNA, the more closely related the species.

🔥 Homologous vs Analogous — KNOW THE DIFFERENCE!

Homologous = same STRUCTURE, different function, SAME ancestor. (Human arm + whale flipper)
Analogous = same FUNCTION, different structure, DIFFERENT ancestor. (Bird wing + butterfly wing — both fly but NOT related)

🏆 4. Natural Selection vs Artificial Selection
NATURAL

🌿 Natural Selection

The environment does the selecting. Individuals with helpful traits survive & reproduce → pass traits to offspring.

Darwin's 4 Steps:

  • Organisms produce MORE offspring than can survive
  • Individuals vary (heritable variation)
  • Best-fit individuals survive & reproduce
  • Change is slow & gradual

Fitness = number of viable offspring you produce. Not about living long — about how many babies survive to reproduce!

ARTIFICIAL

👨‍🌾 Artificial Selection

Humans do the selecting. We choose which traits to breed for.

Examples:

  • 🐄 Cows bred for more milk
  • 🌽 Corn: from 5-12 kernels → 500+ kernels
  • 🥦 Wild mustard → broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower
  • 🐕 Bulldogs bred for flat face → breathing problems!

Monoculture risk: planting only one crop variety = if disease hits, ALL plants die!

GENE POOL

🧬 Gene Pool & Allele Frequency

The gene pool = all alleles in a population. Evolution = change in allele frequencies over generations.

Individuals don't evolve — POPULATIONS evolve!

Example: Grey wolves in the north have more "white coat" alleles in their gene pool than southern wolves.

🎲 5. Factors Changing Allele Frequency

🧬 Mutation

Only source of NEW alleles. Random changes in DNA. If in gametes → passed to next generation. Provides raw material for evolution.

🏃 Gene Flow (Migration)

Movement of alleles between populations due to individuals migrating. Increases genetic diversity in the new population.

Example: A bird from population A moves to population B and breeds → A's alleles now in B's gene pool.

💑 Non-Random Mating

Individuals choose mates based on phenotype (looks/behaviour). Increases homozygous individuals but does NOT change allele frequencies directly.

IMPORTANT!

🎲 Genetic Drift

Random changes in allele frequency due to chance. Big effect in small populations. Two types:

🏝️ Founder Effect: A few individuals start a NEW isolated population → limited gene pool. Example: Amish community has high rate of polydactylism (6th finger)!

🌪️ Bottleneck Effect: Disaster/disease drastically reduces population → survivors have only fraction of original alleles. Example: Pingelap Island typhoon (1775) → only 30 survivors → 10% of current population has color blindness!

🌿 Natural Selection

Most significant factor for new species. Environment selects which traits survive. Changes allele frequencies over many generations → leads to speciation.

🔥 Genetic Drift vs Natural Selection

Drift = RANDOM, due to chance (lottery). Bigger effect in small populations.
Natural Selection = NON-RANDOM, environment actively selects for useful traits.

📊 6. The 3 Types of Natural Selection
⬆️

Stabilizing Selection

Middle/average phenotype is favoured. Extremes are eliminated. Reduces variation.

🍼 Example: Human birth weight. Too small = unhealthy. Too large = hard to deliver. Average is best!

Middle peaks, extremes gone

➡️

Directional Selection

One extreme is favoured. Distribution curve SHIFTS in that direction. Common when environment changes.

🦋 Example: Peppered moths going dark in industrial England. Antibiotic resistance in bacteria!

Shifts to one side →

↔️

Disruptive Selection

BOTH extremes are favoured. Middle is eliminated. Also called diversifying selection.

🐟 Example: Coho salmon — tiny males "sneak" to mate, large males fight for mates. Medium males fail at BOTH!

Two peaks, middle disappears

🌏 7. Speciation & Reproductive Isolation

🔬 What is Speciation?

Formation of new species from existing ones (= macroevolution). Happens when populations become so different they can no longer produce viable, fertile offspring.

Biological species = organisms that CAN interbreed to produce fertile offspring.

🏔️ Allopatric vs Sympatric

Allopatric = population split by geographical barrier (mountain, ocean). They evolve separately until they can't interbreed even if brought back together.

Sympatric = populations in the same area diverge due to chromosomal changes or non-random mating. More common in plants.

🚧 Reproductive Isolating Mechanisms

PRE-ZYGOTIC (before fertilization)

🏠 Habitat Isolation
Same region, different habitats → rarely meet
⏰ Temporal Isolation
Same habitat but breed at different TIMES (seasons/time of day)
🎵 Behavioural Isolation
Different songs, dances, pheromones → won't mate with other species
🔒 Mechanical Isolation
Anatomically incompatible → fertilization fails physically
🥚 Gametic Isolation
Sperm can't survive in other species' reproductive tract → no fertilization

POST-ZYGOTIC (after fertilization)

💀 Hybrid Inviability
Hybrid embryo develops but dies before birth (genetic incompatibility)
🐴 Hybrid Sterility
Hybrid is born BUT is STERILE (can't reproduce)
Example: Horse 🐴 + Donkey 🐴 = Mule 🐴 (sterile!)
😵 Hybrid Breakdown
Hybrids can mate but their offspring are weak or sterile
Easy memory trick:
Pre = PREVENT mating/fertilization
Post = AFTER zygote forms but it fails
🌱 8. Types of Evolution & Speed

🌳 Divergent Evolution

Species that were similar diverge (become more different) as they adapt to different environments.

Example: Darwin's finches — all from one ancestor but evolved different beaks for different foods. Australian marsupials all diverged from one ancestor!

🌊 Convergent Evolution

Different species independently develop similar traits for similar environments — NOT because they're related.

Example: Dolphins (mammal), sharks (fish), ichthyosaurs (reptile) — all streamlined for water but NOT related! Birds & bats both evolved wings independently.

🐌 Gradualism

Darwin's original model. Evolution is slow and steady — many small changes accumulate over millions of years.

Big changes come from many tiny changes adding up.

⚡ Punctuated Equilibrium

Long periods of NO CHANGE (stasis) interrupted by sudden rapid bursts of change. Most change happens right after diverging from parent species.

vs Gradualism: Gradualism = slow constant change; Punctuated Equilibrium = mostly stable with sudden jumps!
